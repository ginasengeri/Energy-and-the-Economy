East Africa Electricity Generation

Period: 2017–2022

Executive Summary

Between 2017 and 2022, East Africa’s electricity generation was consistently dominated by hydropower, accounting for over 60% of total output each year. Renewable sources collectively peaked at nearly 86% of the energy mix in 2020, buoyed by strong hydroelectric performance and stable contributions from “Other renewables,” which include significant geothermal capacity in Kenya. Fossil fuels—primarily gas—contributed around 12–15%, while solar, wind, and bioenergy grew gradually but remained minor components. Coal and nuclear generation remained negligible throughout the period.

Renewable Energy Sources

Hydroelectric power is the single largest source in the region, consistently above 60% each year. “Other renewables,” which include significant geothermal output—particularly from Kenya’s geothermal fields—account for an average of 12–13%. Wind, solar, and bioenergy are growing steadily but still contribute under 10% combined.

Non-Renewable Energy Sources

Natural gas remains the dominant fossil‐based source, making up roughly 12–15% of generation annually. “Other fossil” (diesel, heavy fuel oil) has declined from 7.5% in 2017 to around 4% in 2022. Coal and nuclear generation are negligible in the mix.

Key Insights

From 2017 to 2022, renewables consistently made up the majority of East Africa’s mix, peaking at 85.7% in 2020. Hydro variability drove most year-to-year changes, while geothermal (under “Other renewables”) provided a stable baseline. Wind experienced a boost in 2021 before settling slightly lower. Gas generation rebounded in 2022 after the dip in 2020.

Anomalies & Trends

  • Renewable share peaked in 2020 (85.7%), driven by very strong hydro output that year.
  • Hydro share dipped after 2020, falling from 66.3% to 60.2% by 2022.
  • Gas generation dipped in 2020 but rose sharply to the highest level in 2022 (6.89 TWh).
  • Wind share jumped in 2021 (6.5%) then eased to 5.8% in 2022.

Recommendations

  1. Invest in grid infrastructure to accommodate variable renewables—especially wind and solar—beyond hydropower.
  2. Expand geothermal capacity in Kenya and neighboring countries to stabilize “Other renewables” output.
  3. Enhance gas‐to‐power efficiency to reduce reliance on diesel and heavy fuel oil.
  4. Plan for seasonal hydro variability by integrating battery storage or pumped storage solutions.
  5. Explore regional interconnections to balance surplus hydro in wet years with deficits in dry periods.
