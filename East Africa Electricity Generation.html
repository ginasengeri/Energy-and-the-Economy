This report discusses the structure of electricity generation in East Africa and highlights the system’s continued dependence on hydropower alongside gradual diversification into geothermal, wind and solar.

Summary

Between 2017 and 2022, East Africa’s electricity generation was consistently dominated by hydropower, accounting for over 60% of total output each year. Renewable sources collectively peaked at nearly 86% of the energy mix in 2020, driven by strong hydroelectric performance and stable contributions from geothermal capacity in Kenya. Fossil fuels—primarily gas—contributed around 12–15%, while solar, wind, and bioenergy grew gradually but remained minor components. Coal and nuclear generation remained negligible throughout the period.

Renewable Energy Sources

Hydroelectric power is the single largest source in the region, with Ethiopia and Uganda being major contributors. Geothermal output from Kenya’s fields accounts for an average of 12–13%, providing a stable baseline. Steady growth of wind and solar is mainly concentrated in Kenya, despite still contributing to just over 7% of electricity sources combined.

Non-Renewable Energy Sources

Natural gas remains the dominant fossil‐based source, making up roughly 12–15% of generation annually. Between 96-98% of gas output comes from Tanzania, where it serves as the primary source of electricity. Other fossils including diesel and heavy fuel oil have declined from 7.5% in 2017 to around 4% in 2022. There are currently no nuclear plants in operation in the region.

Key Insights

  • East Africa's electricity system is anchored in hydropower.
  • Natural gas generation is concentrated in Tanzania.
  • Wind and solar generation have more than triples since 2017 (x4.58 and x3.24 respectively)

Implications

The region’s generation structure creates both operational advantages and systemic vulnerabilities.

Hydro dominance is both a flexibility asset and a concentration risk. Hydro has relatively low marginal costs and can adjust output hourly, or faster. It also creates future storage potential, with pumped-storage hydropower (PSH) being the most widely deployed form of electricity storage globally[1]. However, heavy reliance on hydro exposes the system to drought risk and may struggle to keep pace with growing electricity demand.

As wind and solar expand, grid operators must manage added variability and maintain power balance while respecting transmission limits, ramp rates, reserve requirements and capacity constraints. Prices must also create stronger signals to consumers to encourage demand-side response, while protecting revenues that attract investment.

Some countries have surplus hydro, others face deficits. The East African Power Pool (EAPP) is establishing a day-ahead market (DAM) to enable regional trading among its 13 member countries, which will improve reliability by the sharing of reserves and enable access to cheaper hydro and renewables regionally[2]. This reduces reliance on thermal generation (coal, oil and gas), which often relies on imported fuels and has higher marginal costs, and lowers the need for countries to maintain costly individual reserve capacity. The DAM creates incentives for cross-border trade, increasing revenue streams for generators and attracting investor support.

Energy markets have evolved globally in response to increasing renewable penetration and the operational and investment challenges associated with variability, reliability, and capital formation. This has been seen in reforms such as the UK’s 2013 Electricity Market Reform (EMR)[3] and Alberta’s ongoing market restructuring (REM)[4], both aimed at maintaining reliability and investment under increasing renewable penetration. East Africa’s transition is not isolated, but part of a broader global shift toward redesigning electricity markets to manage renewable variability while safeguarding reliability and investment.

In summary, these dynamics imply the following:

  1. Variable renewables reduce concentration but increases flexibility needs.
  2. Geothermal expansion along the East African Rift System (EARS) can provide a stable baseload.
  3. Seasonal hydro variability may be managed by use of pumped storage solutions.
  4. Regional interconnections balances surplus and deficits across countries.
Limitations: This analysis uses aggregated annual generation data and does not capture seasonal variability or short-term reliability events. Reported figures depend on available national reporting and may exclude off-grid generation.
