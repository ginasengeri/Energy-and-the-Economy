Electricity Access in East Africa

Period: 2017–2022

Summary

Between 2017 and 2022 all five focus countries in East Africa achieved steady gains in electricity access. Kenya led with a rise from 55.8% to 76.0% of its population connected, while Rwanda posted the second highest increase (34.1% to 50.6%). Ethiopia, Uganda, and Tanzania each recorded improvements of 11–15 percentage points, driven by a mix of grid extensions and off-grid solutions funded primarily by World Bank programs, AfDB, AFD and other regional development banks.

Key Developments

Ethiopia's network reinforcement under ENREP focused on extending medium-voltage on-grid networks around urban centers and rolling out off-grid mini-grids. Key financing came through the World Bank’s ASCENT and PRIME programs [2], alongside IDA and national utilities, positioning Ethiopia to connect an additional six million people by 2027 [1] and to attract private- sector renewable investment [3]. Kenya’s rapid 20.2-point rise was driven by the Kenya Off-Grid Solar Access Project (KOSAP) for rural areas and systematic grid densification by REREC, with backing from the World Bank, AfDB and EU grants [5], [6]. The country is targeting 100% renewable electricity by 2030 [6], and has adopted a Universal Service Fund strategic plan for 2022–2026 [4]. Rwanda combined a major World Bank energy-sector policy operation [7], with AFD’s EAQIP initiative [8] to push grid lines into northern and western provinces and deploy solar home systems in remote communities, aiming for universal access by 2030. Tanzania leveraged MCC compacts [9] and World Bank Rural Electrification Expansion funding [10] to drive rural grid extensions. The TREEP initiative, backed by World Bank funding, has connected more than 4.5 million rural users during the 2017-2022 period [10]. Complemented by SREP-backed off-grid solar programs, these efforts lay the groundwork for ongoing expansion of household connections. Uganda’s Electricity Access Scale-Up Project (EASP), co-financed by the World Bank IDA and the Clean Technology Fund, is boosting both main grid expansions and solar/hybrid mini-grids in refugee host and rural communities [11], [12].

Highlights and Trends

Kenya stands out both for highest overall access and largest five-year increase [5], reflecting the scale-up of off-grid solar in remote regions and targeted grid upgrades. Rwanda, while starting from a low base, achieved a strong +16.5 pp through policy-backed grid rollout and quality improvements [7], [8]. Grid extensions dominate in Ethiopia and Uganda [2], [11], [12], while Rwanda and Kenya balance grid with off-grid solutions [5], [7], [8]. Ethiopia’s growth (+11.1 pp) remains constrained by its dispersed rural population [2], [3], highlighting the need for more mini-grid and decentralized solutions. Tanzania’s moderate progress under TREEP reflects ongoing financing gaps in its power infrastructure [10]. Both Tanzania and Uganda achieved mid-teens gains largely through donor-backed rural electrification programs [9], [10], [11], [12], pointing to the importance of blended finance in closing remaining access gaps. Private-sector incentives and international credit lines are key drivers in Rwanda and Ethiopia [3],[8].

Recommendations

Ethiopia: Accelerate private-sector participation in renewable mini-grids, streamline licensing for remote communities, and complement IDA support with concessional financing for distributed solar.

Kenya: Scale the USF Strategic Plan and expand last-mile connections through performance-based subsidies and partnerships supporting battery storage deployment.

Rwanda: Integrate mini-grid operators into national grid expansion plans to ensure consistent tariffs, technical standards, and quality of service under EAQIP.

Tanzania: Leverage MCC and World Bank platforms to pilot hybrid solar–diesel mini-grids, reducing dependence on costly grid extensions in sparsely populated areas.

Uganda: Strengthen EASP implementation through flexible financing instruments for solar home systems and viability-gap funding that attracts private mini-grid developers.

Across the region, the following broader actions would reinforce progress:

  1. Scale up off-grid solar and mini-grid deployment in underserved regions, building on Kenya’s model.
  2. Enhance private-sector participation through targeted incentives and streamlined regulatory frameworks.
  3. Advance follow-on phases of ENREP, TREEP, and similar programs to reduce remaining rural access gaps.
  4. Prioritize medium- and low-voltage infrastructure upgrades to improve reliability and system resilience.
  5. Promote regional knowledge sharing on effective financing mechanisms, subsidy schemes, and delivery models.
