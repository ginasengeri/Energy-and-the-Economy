Electricity Access in East Africa

Period: 2017–2022

Executive Summary

Between 2017 and 2022, five East African countries—Ethiopia, Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania and Uganda—achieved significant improvements in electrification rates through a mix of on-grid expansion and off-grid solutions. Kenya led the region with a 20.2-percentage point gain, while Rwanda posted a notable 16.5-point increase from a lower baseline. Multilateral financing from the World Bank, AfDB, AFD and others underpinned grid densification, rural electrification and solar mini-grid rollout, setting each country on a trajectory toward universal access.

Insights

Ethiopia focused on extending medium-voltage on-grid networks around urban centers and rolling out off-grid mini-grids. Key financing came through the World Bank’s ASCENT and PRIME programs alongside IDA and national utilities, positioning Ethiopia to connect an additional six million people by 2027 and to attract private-sector renewable investment.

Kenya’s rapid 20.2-point rise was driven by the Kenya Off-Grid Solar Access Project (KOSAP) for rural areas and systematic grid densification by REREC, with backing from the World Bank, AfDB and EU grants. The country is targeting 100% renewable electricity by 2030 and has adopted a Universal Service Fund strategic plan for 2022–2026.

Rwanda combined a major World Bank energy-sector policy operation with AFD’s EAQIP initiative to push grid lines into northern and western provinces and deploy solar home systems in remote communities, aiming for universal access by 2030.

Tanzania leveraged MCC compacts and World Bank Rural Electrification Expansion funding to drive rural grid extensions. Complemented by SREP-backed off-grid solar programs, these efforts lay the groundwork for ongoing expansion of household connections.

Uganda’s Electricity Access Scale-Up Project (EASP), co-financed by the World Bank IDA and the Clean Technology Fund, is boosting both main grid expansions and solar/hybrid mini-grids in refugee host and underserved rural communities.

Anomalies and Trends

Kenya stands out with the largest five-year increase (+20.2 pp), reflecting the scale-up of off-grid solar in remote regions and targeted grid upgrades. Rwanda, while starting from a low base, achieved a strong +16.5 pp through policy-backed grid rollout and quality improvements.

Ethiopia’s growth (+11.1 pp) remains constrained by its dispersed rural population, highlighting the need for more mini-grid and decentralized solutions. Tanzania and Uganda saw mid-teens gains driven by rural electrification compacts – pointing to the importance of blended finance in bridging infrastructure gaps.

Recommendations

For Ethiopia, accelerating private-sector participation in renewable mini-grids and streamlining licensing will be crucial to reach more remote communities. Continued IDA support should be paired with concessional debt for distributed solar.

Kenya should maintain momentum by scaling the USF Strategic Plan, catalyzing last-mile connections through performance-based subsidies and cross-sector partnerships for battery storage solutions.

Rwanda’s success under EAQIP can be deepened by integrating mini-grid operators into the national grid expansion plan, ensuring uniform tariffs and quality standards.

Tanzania should leverage its MCC and World Bank platforms to pilot hybrid solar-diesel mini-grids, reducing reliance on expensive grid extensions in low-density zones.

Uganda can further scale up its EASP by adopting flexible financing instruments for solar home systems and incentivizing private mini-grid developers through viability gap funding.
